Research Projects

#AntiHaul: Creator Motivation and Anti-Consumption

VURF Program and Undergraduate Research Assistant

I am currently working on this project with a Computer Science research team under Dr. Sarah Cooney. It began as a project through Villanova's Undergraduate Research Fellowship (VURF) during the summer and has continued into the school year as I was hired as an Undergraduate Research Assistant. It focuses on the #antihaul trend on YouTube, expanding on my previous research on #sheinhaul and #thrifthaul. It is categorized as SHCI, a field exploring Sustainable Human-Computer Interaction. I created Python programs utilizing YouTube's Public Data API to create a subset of videos that may be related to #antihaul. Due to the unreliable nature of the API, I then manually reviewed each video to ensure it was properly related to #antihaul and belonged in our dataset. The team each watched these videos and noted video creator motivations informed by previous research. We searched for correlations between creator motivation and viewer engagement, but we found minimal correlation. I created a poster describing this research and presented with my team at Villanova's Undergraduate Research Symposium in November 2024. We have since continued this research, expanding into transcript analysis and vocabulary correlations, as well as utilizing sock puppets to analyze YouTube's recommendation algorithm relating to #antihaul.

May 2024 - Present

#Haul: An Analysis of YouTube Discourse Surrounding Over-Consumption and Sustainability in Haul Videos

MATCH Program
Published Paper

I worked on this project with a Computer Science research team under Dr. Sarah Cooney. It applied through Villanova's First-Year MATCH Program and was chosen by Dr. Cooney to join the team. It focused on the #haul trend on YouTube, specifically examining #sheinhaul and #thrifthaul. It is categorized as SHCI, a field exploring Sustainable Human-Computer Interaction. I created Python programs utilizing YouTube's Public Data API to create two subsets of videos. One subset was meant to be related to #sheinhaul, and the other related to #thrifthaul. Due to the unreliable nature of the API, the team then manually reviewed each video to ensure it was properly related to its related hashtag and belonged in our dataset. The team each watched these videos and noted elements of pro-consumption or anti-consumption. We found these themes in the videos themselves, in the video's description, or from the comments. We analyzed this data with a focus on sustainability on VSPs (video sharing platforms). My team worked to create a full research paper which was submitted and accepted to NordiCHI 2024. It is published at the link above.

Non-Recursive Solution of the Tower of Hanoi

Co-Master Program
Website

I worked on this project with a Mathematics research team under Dr. Alexander Diaz-Lopez. It applied through Villanova's Co-Master Program and was chosen by Dr. Diaz-Lopez to join the team. We explored the Tower of Hanoi and its known, most efficient solution. We aimed to find an equation which could determine the peg that any given disk was on at any given move. As the only Computer Scientist on the team, I took on the role of creating simulations of the game to verify our equation on millions of inputs. I created these programs using Python and shared them with the team. We then proceeded to verify our solution through mathemtical proofs. Our team created a slideshow and presented at the EPaDel conference at Albright College and Villanova's Mathematics Symposium. In addition, I created an informal website where users could input any move number and see the location of every disk in a 50-disk Tower of Hanoi. ss is available at the above link.
